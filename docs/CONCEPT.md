## 1. The problem we are solving

Many people feel lost. The usual story blames technology and economics — widening inequality, a shrinking middle class, the fear that AI will erase the work people built their identity around. Those forces are real. But the feeling underneath them is older and simpler: the sense of **not being needed by anyone nearby**.

The existing social networks were supposed to connect us. Instead, by optimizing for attention and scale, they produced more comparison, more performance, and more isolation. Billions of people are more "connected" than ever and lonelier than ever. That gap is the opportunity — not to build a kinder Facebook, but to serve the human need Facebook structurally cannot.

We are not competing with the attention economy. We are serving what it abandoned: real, local, purpose-driven connection between people who are physically around each other.

## 2. The core idea

Peoplearound applies the logic of open-source collaboration to ordinary human goals. On GitHub, strangers do meaningful work together for free because effort is made visible, attributable, and permanent. Your contributions become your reputation.

Peoplearound does the same for life. A person declares a goal — start a community garden, get fit, launch a small venture, set up a tutoring program, fix up a home. People around them can star it, follow it, and contribute: a skill, an hour, a tool, advice, or simply showing up. Every accepted contribution is credited to the person who made it, confirmed by others, and recorded permanently.

This attacks the feeling of being lost from both sides at once. The person with the goal gains support, momentum, and proof that people care. The contributor — often someone equally adrift — gains the thing that actually restores purpose: being needed, and having that need acknowledged by a specific human.

> Everyone is both helper and helped. Contribution is the currency of worth, and it is earned from others, never self-declared.

## 3. How it works: one loop, end to end

Follow Maria, recently laid off, who posts: *"I want to turn the empty lot on Oak Street into a community garden."*

### The living page

The goal is not a status update that scrolls away. It is a **living page** — like a repository — with a title, a description, a current state, and a history that accumulates over time. The page is the unit of the product, not the post.

### The star

A neighbor stars Maria's goal. A star is low-commitment but not meaningless: it signals "I would be glad this existed." When forty people star the garden, that cluster is real data — the desire exists, Maria is not alone, and she can see it. For someone who feels lost, that first cluster of stars is the moment the system earns its existence. The star's job is to **defeat isolation before any work begins**.

### The contribution

A star is cheap; a contribution is the hard part. Examples:

- Someone shares the city land-use permit form and who to call — knowledge that advances the goal.
- Someone offers a truck to haul soil on Saturday — a committed resource and time.
- Someone with experience offers to help write the city proposal — a skill and hours.

What makes these real rather than performative is that each one moves the project to a **new state**. That is the equivalent of a merged commit. The test for whether something counts is not "did someone claim they helped" but **"did the project actually change because of it."**

### Acknowledgment

The owner accepts a contribution, which credits the contributor — **you cannot credit yourself**; someone else must acknowledge that your effort landed. This single rule kills most performative gaming, because the like economy rewards claiming while this rewards being acknowledged by the person you helped.

### The history

As the project proceeds, the page tells a true story: *idea → forty stars → permit information contributed → proposal written → soil hauled → first planting day → harvest.* Anyone can scroll it. Facebook would have shown the harvest photo with two hundred likes and erased everyone who made it happen. Peoplearound makes the making visible. **The history is the product.**

### What each person walks away with

- **The owner:** a garden — and proof that declaring a goal in public summoned real people.
- **The stargazer:** a small stake, a thing to walk past with quiet pride, and a nudge toward contributing next time.
- **The contributors:** a permanent, attributed record that they were needed and they showed up.

> Your profile is not who you say you are. It is what you have done for the people around you, as confirmed by them.

## 4. Reputation and acknowledgment

The whole system rests on a fair record of what actually happened. Three rules keep that record trustworthy without creating a single point of failure.

### Credit comes from multiple confirmers

The owner accepts a contribution, but it becomes fully credited only when others attest to it — co-contributors who saw it happen, or stargazers who witnessed the result. The owner accepts; the community attests. If an owner is flaky or credit-hungry, the contributor is not stuck: **credit routes around a bad owner.** This is the most important fix for the acknowledgment bottleneck.

### Leaving is normal

A contributor can step away at any time and keep credit for what they actually did, with no penalty and no "abandoned" stigma. The lost people this product serves often cannot commit reliably; a system that punishes leaving will bleed exactly the people it is for.

### Reputation as demonstrated work, not endorsements

Skills are not self-declared or endorsed by friends the way they are on LinkedIn, where endorsements became close to meaningless. Here a skill is proven by acknowledged contributions backed by a real artifact in a project history. Reputation is contextual ("trusted on community projects in this neighborhood"), surfaced only when relevant, and earned from others.

Rank by acknowledged impact, never by volume. **There is no public leaderboard** — it would make helping competitive and shame everyone below the top. People see their own history privately, and reputation surfaces only in context.

## 5. When a goal does not take off

Most goals will fail to attract contributions. Unlike an abandoned code repository, a failed goal has a lonely human attached who declared a hope in public and watched nobody come. Handled carelessly, this would deepen the very lostness the product exists to cure. **How we handle failure matters more than how we handle success.**

### Never display a goal as "failed"

A goal that does not take off is quietly archived or gently reframed — never marked as a public failure.

### The AI agent as gardener, not coroner

An AI agent helps, but its job is to prevent quiet death while there is still time — not to perform an autopsy on a person who was just ignored. Its roles:

- **Before posting** — shape vague goals into contributable ones. "I want to get my life together" becomes "a weekly walking group." Most failures are really malformed goals, and this is the agent's highest-value role.
- **During** — when a project stalls, nudge privately and constructively: "Projects like yours often get moving with one small concrete ask. Want to add one?" A coach, not a judge.
- **If it does not take off** — handle the off-ramp with dignity, offering a smaller version or redirecting the person into a nearby active project. Redirecting a lost person into someone else's succeeding project may be the most healing possible outcome.

The agent learns what makes contribution happen by watching acknowledgment data across thousands of projects, so the credit ledger doubles as the agent's training signal. One mechanism serves both the acknowledgment problem and the failure problem.

> The agent is scaffolding that disappears — like a good teacher working to become unnecessary. Its success metric is human contributions and acknowledgments happening, not agent interactions. If people relate to the AI instead of to each other, the AI has replaced the mission.

## 6. Events: where it becomes physical

Owners create events; contributors join or not, with a single lightweight signal. *"Planting day, Saturday 10am — are you in?"* This is the kind of concrete, low-pressure coordination that gets people physically together.

An RSVP is a coordination signal, never a performance metric. **Presence is rewardable; absence is never punishable.** If someone shows up and contributes, that contribution is acknowledged and credited. If life gets in the way, nothing is held against them. That asymmetry keeps the system humane for the people it serves.

Events connect to everything else: they are the strongest contribution-generating moment, the best defibrillator for a stalling project, and the gentlest on-ramp for an isolated person — joining a one-time Saturday event is far lower-stakes than committing to a whole project.

## 7. What makes this different

- **Not a feed.** Living project pages with permanent histories, not disposable posts.
- **Worth from deeds, not declarations.** Reputation is what you did for people, confirmed by them.
- **Local and real.** Built for people physically around each other, doing tangible things.
- **Helper and helped are the same person.** The system that supports also restores purpose.
- **AI that fades.** The agent connects people, then steps out of the way.

## 8. Open questions and risks

These are the parts most likely to make or break the product, named honestly rather than glossed over.

- **The unit problem.** A code commit either merges or it does not. "I helped with the garden" has no automatic verification. Defining a contribution that is real and attributable without becoming gameable is the central design challenge.
- **Local cold-start.** This is not one network to seed but thousands of tiny local ones, each useless until it reaches critical density. The likely answer: go absurdly deep in one neighborhood, win it completely, then template it.
- **The seriousness gradient.** Self-improvement, ventures, and home projects are very different beasts — some private and emotional, some public and practical. Launching with one category where contribution is most natural is safer than serving all of them poorly.
- **Who pays, and why.** If the lonely person is the product sold to sponsors, this becomes Facebook with a kinder interface. If the lonely person is the customer being served, it is something new. This question must be answered before scale, not after.

## 9. A way to start

"As impactful as Facebook" is an outcome, not a starting strategy. The biggest things started absurdly small and specific. A sensible sequence:

1. Pick one neighborhood and one goal category where contribution is most natural and visible.
2. Prove the loop by hand — a living page, stars, acknowledged contributions, one event — before building much software.
3. Template what worked, then expand to the next neighborhood.
4. Add the AI agent and richer reputation once you understand what people actually show up for.
5. Build the broad platform last — software built before you understand the human behavior tends to fail.

> The mission is not to build a better LinkedIn or a kinder Facebook. It is to restore the feeling of mattering — to make sure that the people around you know you are needed, and you know it too.
